Game Writing


Since 2018, I have been writing professionally for games. I am especially proud of my work on the BAFTA, Nebula, and Hugo award-winning game Baldur’s Gate 3. This includes writing branching dialogues (from the invention of the storylines to the execution of the dialogue and all the different paths the story can take), as well as what we called ‘technical writing’: item and spell descriptions, tutorial messages, UI, etc.

Branching Dialogue


Crusher, the foot-obsessed goblin


I came up with the concept of this scene where the player gets confronted with a nasty goblin who wants them to show subordination. Since they are infiltrating the goblin camp, getting on his bad side might end in a huge fight, but if the player complies, they see that the goblin is wearing a toe ring that they can steal… if they have the stomach for it.

While writing the dialogue, I added a lot of different ways the scene could play out: you can kiss the goblin’s foot, try to steal his toe-ring, or piss him off by licking his toes. Alternatively, you can try to convince the goblin to kiss your feet instead… just to name a few options.

The final product is a collaboration between me, a scripter who handled the technical side of the dialogue, and multiple other writers who supplied the lines for the companion characters who interject in the dialogue (Shadowheart and Astarion in the video above).

The Arcane Tower and Bernard the Automaton


The story I was given for this area was that the tower used to belong to a wizard named Lenore, who had long since passed away. The only ‘living’ thing left in the tower was an automaton she had made, who continued to guard the structure.

My brief was to write a dialogue for this automaton where the player can convince it to stand down. Since convincing guards to let you through was already quite common in the game, and we were dealing with a robot, I came up with the idea that the ‘convincing’ couldn’t be done with the usual persuasion mechanics, but the player would instead need to find a passphrase to unlock the automaton’s peace state. After some brainstorming with my lead and the scripter whom I was working on the situation with, we decided to make the passphrase into a poem that you could find in a book in the tower, and we also added some other poems and passphrases to unlock different perks (and a dangerous ‘attack everything around you’ state for the automaton). Later, we also added a passphrase to ask for a hug for an emotional touch, and to showcase how lonely the wizard was, all alone in her tower.

Because of the lack of characters in the area, items and item descriptions were very important to tell the story of this lonely wizard, as well as to give the player hints on how to dispel traps throughout the tower. Technical Writing


This section encompasses all the game writing I did that is not related to dialogues. Concise, clear language is important for these kinds of texts. I would usually be editing a designer’s text to make sure that their message gets across in as few words as possible, and, where fitting, I would add just a dab of creativity.

One small exception would be item descriptions, where instead of a designer’s words, I would base my text on the image of an item I would be describing. The goal of these descriptions would be to evoke an image or feeling in just one or two sentences, so the player can experience a quick moment of fun without having to spend too much time reading instead of playing.

I have included examples of both beneath.

Thisobald Thorm, the Undead Brewer


Thisobald is a mini-boss who empowers himself by drinking his own concoctions. However, when doing so, he will become more and more drunk. I wrote 5 conditions to communicate to the player which attacks will be the most useful against him, as well as to explain why imps have suddenly joined the fight.
